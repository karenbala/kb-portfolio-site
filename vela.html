PLAY IN ALL KINDS OF WEATHER

Vela is a weather app that lets you play in the water.
Many weather applications provide weather forecasts to their users but not specific to watersports enthusiasts. Watersport users have different weather conditions based on their sport and tolerance for risk.

PROJECT GOALS

Many weather applications provide weather forecasts to their users but not specific to watersports enthusiasts. Watersport users have different weather conditions based on their sport and tolerance for risk.

Vela provides users with accurate information so they can go out in the water with confidence.

PROBLEM STATEMENT

Watersport enthusiasts need an easy solution to understand local weather conditions that are typically shown in complex technical charts so they can plan a safe and enjoyable time in the water.

" Weather forecasts are mainly about applying sunscreen or grabbing your umbrella. "

MARKET RESEARCH

The Competition Space

The primary weather forecasters, weather.com, noaa.gov, and weather.gov have maintained their expertise and authority by being ubiquitous. Digital and non-digital weather outlets use one or all three organization’s information and satellites. Their information to their users are not specific or shared in way that informs water play.

An analogous analysis of Glossier, glossier.com, imparts wisdom to the competition space. They are a thriving company in the health and beauty space. It is a market that large name brand companies lead, but Glossier has created a loyal and enthusiasitic community. They do this by accepting their customers will have many brands of make-up in their bags: not all complexions are the same, and their community will drive their products.

Vela can be an addition to the top 3.

Weather.com is ubiquitous in the weather sector.

Glossier is a new member of the health and beauty market.

Research Goals
  1. What weather forecasting apps do users use before heading out into the water?
  2. How often do they check weather apps before a trip? During the trip?
  3. Have they ever misread a weather forecast in the past? How often? And what could be made better?
Top 5 Needs/Goals
  1. I would love to add layers of information by easily toggling.
  2. I would love a simple rating if it is a nice day to go kayaking.
  3. If I can customize my homescreen that would be great.
  4. Accuracy is important
  5. Imagery is my best friend.
Top 5 Frustrations
  1. Glare is a problem.
  2. Scrolling is hard to find information.
  3. The easiest to misread are the tides.
  4. I exit an app quickly, if I can’t find information fast.
  5. I don’t like reading text, I need images to understand most things.

Continue testing and iterating the design.

Low and mid-fidelity prototyping is meant to be iterative so getting feedback from user participants is very helpful to move the design toward handoff. A moderated remote usability test was conducted with the following goals:

  1. Can participants easily complete the task of setting their optimal weather conditions?
  2. Can participants quickly and easily customize their homescreen?
  3. Sharing water trip plans to family and friends for safety
  4. Are participants able to plan trips and share them with family and friends?

TEST: Usability Test

Usability Test Context:

Six participants for a moderated remote usability test. All watersport enthusiasts with an age range of 20s-50s.

Test Issues #1: High severity

Missing “share” button when planning a trip.

Action: Create a “share” button after saving a trip

Evidence: The ability to share trips with friends and family is a unique feature from all the weather apps in the market. Finally, during all user interviews, 75% of participant’s feedback want the ability to share trips with family and friends.

Before

After: Share button added

Test Issues #2: High severity

Missing “location” field for watersport play alert.

Action: Create a “location” field for watersport play alerts.

Evidence: 100% of user interviews discussed this key feature, the ability to receive a watersport play alert when a user’s location and weather conditions align. This feature is not found in other weather apps giving Vela a unique position in the market.

After: Adding a location field.

Test Issues #3: Medium severity

Missing signifier to help users proceed in trip planning.

Action: Create a “continue to home” and/or “create another trip”button.

Evidence: 100% of all test participants did not know how to proceed to the next screen.

After: Share button added

Lessons Learned

Aileen and Nori and Everyone.During this phase of the project, I was quite happy with the design. I felt I was designing toward the problem statement’s solution. But I was only thinking of a population that mirrors my physical and mental abilities.

The next section of the development of Vela shows how accessibility in design is critical for a wider audience to engage in products and services.

REFINE: Accessibility

Design for Accessibility:

Information and design decisions in this section are from:

  • WCAG 2.1
  • US Web Design Systems
  • WebAim

Conveying Information with Color

  • Created an “error code” with warning symbols so users can see an error has been made
  • Removed a background logo symbol that distracted users and did not pass the 4.5:1 ratio.
  • Ranking: AA

Conveying Information with Color

  • Created a color for “information state” as suggested in US Web Design Systems. A color that works well with the Vela Design Language
  • Ranking: AA

Color Contrast

  • Modified the primary color for modals to achieve the 4.5:1 ratio.
  • Ranking: AA, AAA

Form Labels and Placeholders

  • Helping users with labels and placeholders will prevent confusion and frustration

Audio and Voice

  • Live audio of forecasted weather for users who are visually impaired
  • Voice activated input for users who cannot type or touch on the interface
